I work on the machine learning behind Zepto, India's largest quick-commerce company. Most of my time goes to a problem that is simple to state and stubborn to solve: given a few hundred dark stores, a catalogue that turns over constantly, and customers who expect their order in ten minutes, what should each store hold, how much should we buy, and how should it all move? Underneath those questions sit demand forecasting, replenishment, routing, and assortment planning. It is the quiet machinery that decides whether the thing you wanted is on the shelf when you open the app.

I came to this from physics, by way of telescopes. My master's thesis, with the CTLearn group at the Universidad Complutense de Madrid, used recurrent networks to clean up the noisy images that gamma-ray telescopes record when cosmic particles strike the atmosphere. Before that I spent a Google Summer of Code on the same project, and I studied electrical engineering and mathematics at BITS Pilani. Somewhere along the way I stopped pointing models at the sky and started pointing them at supply chains, which have more in common than you would think: both are mostly the work of pulling a signal out of a great deal of noise.

The work I care about most sits where a model meets the real world: a truck that leaves at six, a shelf that holds only so much. Most of what I know I learned by shipping things and watching them break in instructive ways.
