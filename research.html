Gamma-ray astronomy

Master's thesis · Universidad Complutense de Madrid · 2022–23

Imaging atmospheric Cherenkov telescopes never photograph a gamma ray directly. They catch the faint flash of light it makes when it strikes the upper atmosphere and starts a cascade of particles, and they read the physics back out of that shower. The images are noisy, and the noise hides the signal. Working with the CTLearn group under Daniel Nieto, I used recurrent neural networks to denoise these shower images, so that what survives is closer to the event that actually happened.

Google Summer of Code · OpenAstronomy / CTLearn · 2020

Much of the data from telescopes already in operation comes in ROOT, a particle-physics file format that CTLearn could not read. I spent the summer teaching it to, which quietly opened the tool up to a whole generation of existing instruments, and I trained deep networks to do one of the field's basic jobs: telling a real gamma-ray image apart from the far more common cosmic-ray background. Mentored by Tjark Miener, Daniel Nieto, and Ari Brill.

Other research

Maritime Research Center · 2020

A short project with a long reach. Using deep learning on AIS tracking data, I worked on predicting where ships in the Indian Ocean were headed, flagging anomalous behaviour, and assessing risk to marine protected areas, down to whether vessels might be routed more kindly around recorded marine-mammal sightings.

Sky Watch Array Network (SWAN) · 2019–22

SWAN is an effort to build a distributed radio array in India, a possible precursor to the Square Kilometre Array. Its ambition is as much human as technical: to grow a community of radio astronomers in the country alongside the instrument itself. I was involved through its early years.

Solar flares and ionospheric disturbances · 2019

An early project, and a fond one. We set out to predict and classify solar flares and other ionospheric disturbances from SWPC GOES X-ray flux, some of which we gathered ourselves: a loop antenna and SID software, listening for the small dips a flare leaves in distant radio signals, all stitched together with Python.

Along the way: a stint on the core committee of the Radio Astronomy Club at BITS Pilani, and a good weekend volunteering at the LIGO-India exhibit at Vigyan Samagam, explaining gravitational waves to schoolchildren.
